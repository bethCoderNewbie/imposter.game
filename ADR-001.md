This is a fantastic concept. Digitizing social deduction games like Werewolf or Mafia using a "Jackbox-style" architecture (a shared public screen + private mobile devices) solves the biggest pain points of the physical game: needing a dedicated human moderator, complicated night-phase logistics, and rules confusion. 

Here is a comprehensive UX/UI design strategy for bringing this digital Werewolf/Mafia experience to life.

---

### 1. The Core Architecture
* **The Display TV (Public Screen):** Acts as the automated Game Master/Host. It drives the narrative, manages timers, displays public voting, and builds tension. 
* **The Mobile Phone (Private Screen):** Acts as the player's secret identity card, action pad, and voting tool. 

---

### 2. The Display TV (Public Screen) UX/UI

The TV interface should be highly visual, thematic, and readable from a distance.

* **Pre-Game Lobby:**
    * **UI:** A massive, easy-to-scan QR code and a short URL (e.g., `play.wolf.tv`) with a 4-letter room code.
    * **Visuals:** As players join on their phones, their chosen avatars/selfies pop onto the screen around a virtual "campfire" or "village square."
* **The Night Phase (Tension Building):**
    * **UI:** The screen goes dark (e.g., a moonlit village). A large, dramatic countdown timer appears.
    * **Narrative Prompts:** Instead of showing who is doing what, display atmospheric text: *"The Werewolves are hunting..."*, *"The Seer is peering into the future..."*
    * **Audio:** This is crucial for the TV. Play ambient crickets, howling winds, or tense heartbeat music to mask any sounds players might make while tapping their phones.
* **The Day Phase (Information & Debate):**
    * **UI:** The sun rises. The screen announces the night's casualties with dramatic flair.
    * **The Village Layout:** Show a grid of all player avatars. Dead players are grayed out with a "claw mark" or "tombstone" over their picture.
    * **Discussion Timer:** A prominent timer (e.g., 3 minutes) to keep the debate moving. 
    * **Live Voting:** When voting starts, show lines or arrows connecting players to who they are voting for, creating a visual "web of suspicion."
* **Game Over Screen:**
    * **UI:** A celebratory splash screen declaring the winning team (Villagers or Werewolves).
    * **Post-Match Stats:** Show a timeline of the game, highlighting key moments (e.g., "Sarah healed John on Night 2," "Dave betrayed his fellow wolf"). 

---

### 3. The Mobile Phone (Player Device) UX/UI

The mobile UI must prioritize **secrecy**, **clarity**, and **anti-cheating**. 

* **Onboarding & Lobby:**
    * **UI:** Simple input field for Name and a quick selfie/avatar selector. 
* **Role Reveal:**
    * **UX:** A "Hold to Reveal" button. The player must physically hold their thumb on the screen to see their role. If they let go, the screen blanks out. This prevents accidental reveals to neighbors.
    * **UI:** Clear iconography. "You are the SEER." A brief, bulleted list of what the role does.
* **The Night Phase (Crucial Anti-Cheat Design):**
    * **The "Fake Out" Screen:** *Every* player's screen must look identical at a glance to bystanders. Whether you are a Villager with no night action or a Werewolf, the screen should be uniformly dark.
    * **Action UI:** For active roles (Wolves, Seer, Doctor), provide a simple carousel or grid of living players to select.
    * **Haptic Feedback:** Use phone vibrations to confirm actions rather than bright visual flashes. 
    * **Decoy Actions:** Give plain Villagers a "fake" task (like tapping sheep to count them) so they are also tapping their screens. If only the Wolves are tapping, the people sitting next to them will know.
* **The Day Phase (Voting & Notes):**
    * **UI:** A clean list of living players. A simple "Vote to Eliminate" button next to each name.
    * **Private Notepad:** A small text box or toggle system where players can mark players as "Sus" or "Safe" for their own private reference. 
* **The "Dead" State:**
    * **UX:** When a player dies, their screen turns gray. 
    * **Spectator Perks:** Now that they are out, their app reveals everyone's true roles. They can watch the chaos unfold with full knowledge, keeping them engaged even when eliminated.

---

### 4. Key UX Considerations for Social Deduction

* **Pacing & Auto-Skipping:** If all active roles have locked in their night actions early, the TV should automatically skip the rest of the night timer so the game doesn't drag.
* **Disconnect Recovery:** Party games suffer from dropped Wi-Fi. If a player refreshes their browser or app, they should instantly reconnect to their exact game state using browser cookies or a unique token.
* **Accessibility:** Use color-blind friendly palettes (don't rely solely on Red=Bad, Green=Good). Use distinct shapes/icons for different teams.

Here is the step-by-step UX/UI flow for how the Werewolves silently agree on a target.

### User Flow: The Secret Night Hunt

**Context:** It is Night 1. There are two Werewolves (Player A and Player B) and several Villagers.

#### Step 1: The Night Transition
* 📺 **Display TV:** The screen transitions to a dark, moonlit forest. A subtle, rumbling audio track plays to mask the sound of tapping on phones. A 60-second "Night Phase" countdown timer begins.
* 📱 **All Phones:** Every player's phone vibrates simultaneously (haptic feedback). Every screen goes dark to ensure nosy neighbors can't glance over and see a brightly colored "Action" screen.

#### Step 2: The Awakening & Identification
* 📱 **Villager Phones:** Show a dark screen with a decoy interaction. *"Shhh... the village is sleeping. Count the shooting stars."* (Players can tap the screen to count stars, ensuring they are also physically interacting with their phones).
* 📱 **Werewolf Phones (Players A & B):** * A prompt appears: *"Wake up, Werewolves."*
    * **Crucial UI Element:** The top of the screen clearly displays the avatars/names of the other Werewolves. *(e.g., "Your Pack: [Avatar of Player B]")*. 

#### Step 3: The Silent Proposal
* 📱 **Werewolf Phones:** Below the pack information, a grid of the remaining *living* non-wolf players is displayed. 
* **Action:** Player A decides they want to eliminate "Villager Dave." Player A taps Dave's portrait.
* **UI Feedback:** Dave's portrait gets a faint red outline on Player A's screen.

#### Step 4: The Live Synchronization
* 📱 **Player B's Phone:** Instantly, a small icon of Player A's avatar appears on Villager Dave's portrait on Player B's screen. 
* **UX Reality:** Player B now silently knows: *"Ah, Player A wants to kill Dave."*
* **The Choice:** * *Option 1 (Agree):* Player B taps Villager Dave as well.
    * *Option 2 (Disagree):* Player B taps "Villager Sarah" instead. Now, Player A sees Player B's avatar appear on Sarah. They must silently negotiate by changing their votes until their avatars stack on the same target.

#### Step 5: The Lock-In 
* 📱 **Werewolf Phones:** Once both Player A and Player B have tapped "Villager Dave," the UI registers a consensus. A "Confirm Kill" button illuminates at the bottom of the screen.
* **Action:** Either player can tap "Confirm."
* **UI Feedback:** Dave's portrait is slashed with claw marks. The screen fades to dark text: *"Target acquired. Go back to sleep."*

#### Step 6: Edge Case Resolution (The Timer Runs Out)
* *What happens if they are stubborn and don't agree before the 60 seconds are up?*
* **UX Fallback:** If the timer hits zero and the wolves have split votes, the system auto-resolves to prevent the game from breaking. It can randomly select between the tied targets, or, for a more punishing game, result in a "failed hunt" where nobody dies that night.

---

### Why this design works:
1.  **Zero Eye Contact Needed:** The live-updating avatars on the target grid allow the wolves to "talk" and negotiate purely through the UI. 
2.  **Anti-Meta Gaming:** Because the Villagers have a decoy task (tapping shooting stars), the physical action of tapping a screen doesn't give away who the active Werewolves are.
